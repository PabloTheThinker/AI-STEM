Non-phase-locked groups have misaligned windows -- communication is blocked\n\n**Selective attention works by phase-locking**: When you attend to a stimulus, its cortical representation increases gamma frequency by ~3Hz and entrains downstream areas, winning the competition for communication.\n\n### Multi-Frequency Architecture\n\n- **Theta (4-8Hz)**: Samples different stimuli/memory items sequentially\n- **Alpha/Beta (8-30Hz)**: Top-down control, inhibition of irrelevant processing\n- **Gamma (30-100Hz)**: Bottom-up feature binding and local computation\n- **Cross-frequency coupling**: Gamma bursts are nested within theta/alpha cycles, creating a hierarchical temporal code\n\n**Architectural principle:** The mesh needs a clock/oscillation system. Subsystems that should communicate need to be phase-synchronized. A \"tick\" creates alternating send/receive windows. Attention (priority) is implemented by frequency/phase control -- attended subsystems get faster oscillation and preferential phase-locking to downstream targets. Different \"frequencies\" can carry different types of information simultaneously.\n\n---\n\n## 8. PREDICTIVE CODING (FREE ENERGY PRINCIPLE)\n\n### Friston's Free Energy Principle\n\nThe brain minimizes **variational free energy** -- a tractable upper bound on surprise (negative log evidence):\n\n```\nF = E_q[log q(s) - log p(s, o)] >= -log p(o)\n```\n\nWhere `q(s)` is the brain's approximate posterior over hidden states, `p(s,o)` is the generative model, and `p(o)` is the evidence (marginal likelihood of observations).\n\nMinimizing F can happen two ways:\n1. **Perception**: Update internal model to better predict sensory input (change q)\n2. **Action**: Change the world to match predictions (change o)\n\n### Hierarchical Predictive Coding\n\nThe cortex is organized as a hierarchy where each level:\n- Sends **predictions** (top-down) to the level below\n- Receives **prediction errors** (bottom-up) from the level below\n\n```\nPrediction error: epsilon_i = x_i - g_i(x_{i+1})\nState update:     dx_i/dt = D*x_i - partial(epsilon_i)/partial(x_i) + epsilon_{i+1} * partial(g_{i+1})/partial(x_i)\n```\n\nWhere `g_i` is the generative model at level i, and D is a temporal derivative operator.\n\n### The Key Insight for AI\n\n**Only prediction errors propagate upward.** If a higher level successfully predicts a lower level's state, the prediction error is zero, and no signal needs to flow upward. This creates massive compression: you only communicate what is surprising/unexpected.\n\n**Precision weighting**: Each prediction error is weighted by its expected **precision** (inverse variance). High-precision errors (reliable signals) get amplified; low-precision errors (noise) get suppressed. Attention is literally the process of increasing precision weights on relevant prediction errors.\n\n**Architectural principle:** Each subsystem should generate predictions about other subsystems' states. Only prediction errors (deviations from expected state) should propagate through the mesh. This massively reduces communication bandwidth. Precision weighting determines which errors matter -- this is the computational form of attention. The system learns a generative model of its own operation.\n\n---\n\n## 9. MEMORY CONSOLIDATION\n\n### The Two-Stage Model\n\n1. **Encoding (hippocampus)**: New experiences are rapidly encoded in the hippocampus using sparse, pattern-separated representations. This is fast but capacity-limited.\n\n2. **Consolidation (sleep)**: During slow-wave sleep (SWS), memories are replayed and gradually transferred to neocortical networks:\n   - **Sharp-wave ripples** (150-200Hz) in hippocampus trigger replay of recent experience sequences\n   - **Sleep spindles** (12-15Hz) in thalamus gate information flow to cortex\n   - **Slow oscillations** (<1Hz) in cortex create alternating UP states (excitable) and DOWN states (silent)\n   - The three rhythms nest hierarchically: ripples within spindles within slow oscillations\n\n3. **Integration**: Neocortical representations are gradually updated through repeated replay, integrating new memories with existing knowledge structures (schema).\n\n### Emotional Priority\n\nThe **amygdala** modulates consolidation:\n- During encoding: Amygdala activation during emotional events triggers norepinephrine release, strengthening hippocampal encoding\n- During sleep: Amygdala cells representing aversive/salient stimuli show stronger reactivation, biasing the replay toward emotionally significant memories\n- REM sleep specifically consolidates emotional memories, potentially stripping emotional intensity while preserving informational content\n\n### The Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) Theory\n\nMcClelland, McNaughton, O'Reilly (1995): The hippocampus and cortex form complementary learning systems:\n- **Hippocampus**: Fast learning, pattern separation, episodic specifics\n- **Cortex**: Slow learning, pattern completion, statistical regularities/schemas\n\nInterleaved replay during sleep prevents **catastrophic interference** -- the problem where new learning destroys old memories. By replaying old memories alongside new ones, the cortex can integrate both without catastrophic overwriting.\n\n**Architectural principle:** The mesh needs two memory systems: a fast-write buffer (recent interactions, events, context) and a slow-write long-term store (consolidated patterns, behavioral policies, identity). During \"sleep\" (maintenance windows), the fast buffer replays into the slow store. Emotionally/operationally significant memories get priority replay. Old memories are interleaved with new to prevent catastrophic forgetting.\n\n---\n\n## 10. EMBODIED COGNITION AND INTEROCEPTION\n\n### Craig's Model of the Insular Cortex\n\nA.D. (Bud) Craig mapped the neural pathway for interoception -- the sense of the body's internal state:\n\n1. **Lamina I neurons** in the spinal cord carry signals about temperature, pain, itch, muscle tension, visceral state, hunger, thirst\n2. These project to the **dorsal posterior insula** -- the primary interoceptive cortex, creating a detailed map of body state\n3. This representation is progressively integrated as it moves forward through the insula\n4. The **right anterior insula** produces a \"meta-representation\" -- a global feeling of \"how I feel right now\"\n\nThis meta-representation in the anterior insula is what Craig argues is the neural substrate for **subjective emotional awareness** -- the feeling of being a sentient entity.\n\n### The Body as Cognitive Foundation\n\nKey insight: Emotions are not abstract computational states. They are **perceptions of body state**:\n- Fear = perception of rapid heartbeat, muscle tension, shallow breathing\n- Disgust = perception of nausea, stomach contraction\n- Joy = perception of expansive posture, relaxed muscles, warmth\n\nThe anterior insula integrates:\n- Interoceptive signals (body state)\n- Exteroceptive context (what's happening in the world)\n- Memory and prediction (what body state to expect)\n\nThis produces **homeostatic emotions** -- feelings that motivate adaptive behavior (thirst motivates drinking, pain motivates withdrawal).\n\n### The Insular Cortex as Integration Hub\n\nThe insular cortex acts as an interface between:\n- Sensation (what the body feels)\n- Emotion (the affective interpretation)\n- Cognition (the conscious awareness and decision-making)\n\nIt integrates interoceptive signals with other inputs to form \"a common neural substrate for embodied and experiential processes.\"\n\n**Architectural principle:** The mesh needs internal state monitoring -- not just what the system is doing, but how the system IS. Resource levels, processing load, error rates, connection health, thermal state. These \"interoceptive\" signals should be integrated into a global \"feeling state\" that drives adaptive behavior: stressed (high error rates) -> reduce load; healthy (low errors, high coherence) -> take on more. The self-model is not just cognitive -- it is grounded in the system's physical/operational state.\n\n---\n\n## SYNTHESIS: ARCHITECTURAL PRINCIPLES FOR A LIVING MESH\n\nDrawing from all 10 domains,
