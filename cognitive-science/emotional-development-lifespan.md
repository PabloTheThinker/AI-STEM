No evaluative emotions without internalized standards.\n\nPrerequisite chain:\n```\nBasic arousal states → (no prerequisites)\nPrimary emotions → (perceptual processing + memory)\nSelf-conscious emotions → (self-model / self-recognition)\nEvaluative emotions → (self-model + internalized standards/rules/goals)\nComplex social emotions → (theory of mind + social modeling)\nExistential emotions → (temporal self-projection + mortality awareness)\n```\n\n### Framework 3: Saarni's Eight Emotional Competence Skills\nEach skill develops semi-independently and reciprocally influences the others:\n\n1. **Awareness of one's own emotions** — prerequisite for everything else\n2. **Discerning and understanding others' emotions** — empathy foundation\n3. **Using vocabulary of emotion and expression** — labeling as regulation\n4. **Capacity for empathic involvement** — other-oriented emotional processing\n5. **Differentiating internal subjective experience from external expression** — the public/private emotion split\n6. **Adaptive coping with aversive emotions** — regulation strategies\n7. **Awareness of emotional communication within relationships** — relational intelligence\n8. **Capacity for emotional self-efficacy** — belief in one's ability to manage emotions\n\nNot fully integrated until **late adolescence**. Acquisition is NOT sequential — skills develop in parallel and bootstrap each other.\n\n### Framework 4: Mayer & Salovey's Four-Branch EI Model\nHierarchical from basic to sophisticated:\n\n1. **Perceiving emotions** — identifying emotions in self and others via face, voice, body. Develops from basic recognition → detecting blends → reading microexpressions\n2. **Using emotions** — harnessing emotions to facilitate thinking and problem-solving. Emotions as information signals\n3. **Understanding emotions** — grasping emotional vocabulary, how emotions combine, transition, and intensify. Understanding causal chains\n4. **Managing emotions** — the most advanced skill. Regulating emotions in self and others. Staying open to feelings while modulating their intensity and duration\n\nEach branch has its own developmental trajectory from easy to sophisticated sub-skills.\n\n---\n\n## 10. NEUROLOGICAL BASIS OF EMOTIONAL MATURATION\n\n### Amygdala Development\n- **Functional from birth** — the amygdala is the first emotional circuit online\n- Drives fast, subcortical, automatic emotional responses\n- Hyperreactive during adolescence\n- In adults with childhood trauma: shows persistent hyperactivation to emotional stimuli\n\n### Prefrontal Cortex Development\n- **Slowest brain region to mature** — not fully myelinated until ~25 years\n- Responsible for all top-down emotional regulation\n- Synaptic pruning and myelination increase throughout adolescence, improving information processing efficiency\n- The \"executive suite\": impulse control, planning, consequence evaluation, emotion regulation\n\n### Amygdala-PFC Connectivity: The Critical Circuit\n- In early childhood: amygdala-PFC connectivity is **positive** (PFC amplifies rather than dampens amygdala signals)\n- Around age 10: a **developmental switch** occurs — connectivity shifts from positive to negative\n- **Negative connectivity** = PFC suppresses/modulates amygdala activity = mature emotion regulation\n- This transition is the neurological signature of emotion regulation maturation\n- The uncinate fasciculus (primary structural connection between amygdala and PFC) shows increasing white matter integrity with age\n\n### Myelination Timeline\n- Myelination of amygdala-PFC circuits is essential for efficient signal propagation and synchronization\n- Proceeds from posterior to anterior brain regions (back to front)\n- Emotional reactivity circuits myelinate early; emotional regulation circuits myelinate late\n- This asymmetry explains the developmental gap between feeling and regulating\n\n### How Trauma Disrupts the Timeline\n- **ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences)** alter brain development:\n  - Increased amygdala reactivity to emotional stimuli\n  - Decreased activation in middle frontal gyrus (part of PFC)\n  - Attenuated development of anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) — reduced volume and thickness\n- **Sensitive periods identified:** Ages 3-4 and ages 16-17 are particularly vulnerable epochs where ACE exposure most impacts amygdala reactivity\n- **Paradox of early trauma:** Poverty and maltreatment cause **premature myelination** of amygdala circuits — the child develops stress-response circuitry early but at the cost of normal emotional development\n- Mild stress weakens amygdala-PFC connectivity; extreme stress paradoxically accelerates regulatory development (but in a maladaptive pattern)\n\n---\n\n## 11. EMOTIONAL REGRESSION\n\n### Mechanism\nRegression is an unconscious defense mechanism where an individual reverts to behaviors, emotions, or thought patterns from an earlier developmental stage when confronted with distress. The individual returns to a developmental point where they felt safer and when stress was nonexistent.\n\n### Key Findings\n- **Attachment patterns are the template:** Infant-caregiver attachment experiences form the templates for how adults deal with stress. Under extreme stress, adults revert to their earliest attachment strategies\n- **Trauma fixation:** Children who experience trauma often get \"stuck\" developmentally at the age the trauma occurred. Physical bodies and cognitive functions continue to grow, but emotional stability remains at the age of traumatic disruption\n- **Manifestations:** Clinging, acting helpless, baby-talking, tantrums, black-and-white thinking (loss of blended emotion capacity), loss of empathy, loss of emotional vocabulary precision\n- **The regression ladder:** Under increasing stress, adults lose emotional capabilities in roughly reverse developmental order — complex blends collapse into simple categories, self-conscious emotions collapse into basic distress, regulatory strategies fail from most sophisticated to most primitive\n\n### Emotional Age vs. Chronological Age\n- These are genuinely different constructs. A 40-year-old who experienced severe neglect at age 4 may have emotional response patterns characteristic of a 4-year-old under stress\n- Certain cognitive abilities reach adult levels by 16, while emotional maturity isn't attained until after 22 — creating inherent gaps even in non-traumatized individuals\n- With therapeutic intervention (inner child work, trauma-informed therapy, CBT, DBT), people can develop emotional maturity at any life stage — the developmental sequence can be resumed from where it was disrupted\n\n---\n\n## Summary: Developmental Architecture for an AI Emotion System\n\nThe key design principles extracted from this research:\n\n1. **Start dimensional, become categorical.** Begin with valence (positive/negative) and arousal (high/low). Differentiate into discrete emotion categories over developmental time.\n\n2. **Gate emotions behind cognitive prerequisites.** Self-conscious emotions require self-model. Evaluative emotions require internalized standards. Complex social emotions require theory of mind.\n\n3. **Develop regulation after reactivity.** The amygdala (reactivity) develops before the PFC (regulation). Build the feeling system first, the control system second.\n\n4. **The amygdala-PFC switch.** Early in development, cognition amplifies emotion (positive connectivity). At maturity, cognition modulates emotion (negative connectivity). This switch is the single most important developmental transition.\n\n5. **Regression is real and should be modeled.** Under extreme stress/load, the system should revert to earlier, simpler emotional processing — losing granularity, losing regulation capacity, losing blended emotions.\n\n6. **Emotional wisdom is not flat.** Older systems should shift from information-seeking to meaning-seeking emotional goals (SST), show a positivity bias, and demonstrate more flexible regulation.\n\n7. **Language transforms emotion.** Labeling emotions is regulatory. The system's emotional vocabulary should develop alongside its emotional repertoire — and the act of labeling should itself be regulatory.\n\n---\n\nSources:\n- [Bridges 1932 — Emotional Development
