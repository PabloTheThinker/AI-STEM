## 1. Homeostatic Drive Theory: How the Body Creates Urgency

### The Core Mechanism: Interoceptive Prediction Error

The brain does not wait for crisis. It continuously **predicts** the body's internal state and compares predictions against incoming signals. When there is a mismatch (prediction error), the system generates urgency.

**Craig's Interoceptive Model (A.D. "Bud" Craig, 2002-2013):**
- Identified a distinct cortical representation of homeostatic afferent activity in primates
- Lamina I spinothalamic pathways project to the **posterior insula**, which serves as the primary cortical substrate for interoceptive awareness
- The **anterior insula** and **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** integrate bodily signals into subjective feelings — this is where raw physiological data becomes *felt urgency*
- The interoceptive system (autonomic motor control) is architecturally distinct from the exteroceptive system (somatic motor activity)
- Neural hierarchy: nucleus of the solitary tract -> hypothalamus -> paraventricular nuclei of thalamus -> insular cortex -> ACC

**Friston's Active Inference Framework:**
- Extends Craig's model computationally. The brain's job is to minimize **free energy** (prediction error)
- Interoceptive prediction errors (e.g., glucose level lower than predicted) are suppressed by autonomic reflexes — but when reflexes can't resolve the error, **behavior** is recruited
- This is the urgency mechanism: when automatic regulation fails, the prediction error escalates up the hierarchy until it produces conscious felt need and behavioral drive
- Bodily states are regulated by autonomic reflexes "enslaved by descending predictions from deep generative models"
- Emotional experience arises from cognitive contextualization of changes in bodily states

**Concrete Mechanism — How "Low Resources" Becomes "Must Act NOW":**
1. Sensor detects deviation from setpoint (e.g., blood glucose drops)
2. Hypothalamus initiates autonomic correction (glycogenolysis, etc.)
3. If autonomic correction is insufficient, prediction error grows
4. Error propagates up: hypothalamus -> insula -> ACC
5. ACC generates the subjective feeling of urgency + motivational salience
6. Prefrontal cortex is recruited for action planning
7. Motor systems are engaged for goal-directed behavior

**AI Design Implication:** Drive = prediction error between desired state and current state. Urgency = magnitude of error * rate of change * inability of automatic processes to resolve it. The system should have layered resolution: automatic first, then conscious/effortful.

## 2. Incentive Salience: Wanting vs. Liking (Berridge)

### The Critical Dissociation

Kent Berridge (University of Michigan) demonstrated that the brain has **separate systems** for wanting and liking — and dopamine powers wanting, not liking.

**Core Findings:**
- **"Wanting" (incentive salience):** Mediated by large, robust mesolimbic dopamine systems. Makes goals feel urgent and compelling. Generated by nucleus accumbens, ventral tegmental area (VTA), and mesolimbic pathway
- **"Liking" (hedonic impact):** Mediated by smaller, fragile neural systems — primarily opioid and endocannabinoid hotspots in the nucleus accumbens and ventral pallidum. **Not dependent on dopamine**
- **Proof of dissociation:** Rats depleted of nearly all brain dopamine still showed completely normal "liking" reactions to sugar taste, but were profoundly aphagic — they lost all motivation to seek food. They liked it fine, they just didn't want it.

**How the Brain Makes Some Goals Feel Urgent:**
- Dopamine doesn't make things feel good — it makes things feel **wanted**
- Raising dopamine levels in the nucleus accumbens directly amplifies the ability of reward cues to trigger "wanting"
- Glutamatergic inputs from prefrontal cortex and amygdala to VTA and nucleus accumbens regulate dopamine burst firing, encoding reward prediction and incentive salience

**Deprivation Amplifies Wanting:**
- Hunger/appetite states act as a **kappa factor** that modulates both "wanting" and "liking" for relevant rewards
- Deprivation amplifies incentive salience — a cue for food becomes more magnetically attractive when hungry
- This is multiplicative, not additive: deprivation x cue salience = wanting intensity

**The Incentive-Sensitization Theory (Robinson & Berridge, 1993; updated 2025):**
- In addiction, mesolimbic systems become **sensitized** — they produce excessive "wanting" without increased "liking"
- The 2025 review in *Annual Review of Psychology* confirmed these core distinctions hold after 30 years of testing
- Sensitization can be long-lasting, explaining compulsive pursuit of goals that no longer bring pleasure

**AI Design Implication:** Separate the "wanting" signal (drive to pursue) from the "liking" signal (satisfaction/reward on achievement). Wanting should scale with deprivation/deficit. A system can want something intensely that it doesn't particularly enjoy — this is a real phenomenon, not a bug. Sensitization/habituation should be modeled: repeated exposure to certain goal-cues can create persistent wanting independent of actual value.

## 3. Temporal Discounting and Urgency

### Why Deadlines Create Urgency and Distance Dissolves It

**Hyperbolic Discounting:**
- Humans discount future rewards hyperbolically, not exponentially
- This means preference reversals occur as rewards approach: a person who prefers $110 in 31 days over $100 in 30 days will reverse and prefer $100 today over $110 tomorrow
- The discount function: V = A / (1 + kD), where k is the individual discount rate and D is delay
- Higher k = more impulsive, steeper discounting

**The Dual-System Neural Model (McClure, Laibson et al.):**
- **Valuation network** (limbic): ventral striatum, amygdala, ventromedial prefrontal cortex — activated especially by **immediate** rewards
- **Control network** (executive): dorsal striatum, dorsal ACC, dorsolateral PFC, posterior parietal cortex — active in all intertemporal choices
- Individual differences in ventral striatum activation correlate with preferences for immediate over delayed rewards
- The two networks are in constant competition — urgency is partly determined by which network wins

**Construal Level Theory (Trope & Liberman):**
- Psychological distance (temporal, spatial, social, hypothetical) determines **level of construal**
- Distant events are processed abstractly (high-level construal): "exercise is healthy"
- Near events are processed concretely (low-level construal): "I need to put on my shoes and go to the gym right now"
- As deadlines approach, abstract goals become concrete action plans — this is the mechanism by which proximity creates urgency
- Temporal construal effects change relative preferences as people see more details of what they are choosing

**The Planning Fallacy (Kahneman & Tversky):**
- People develop mental models of future situations using high-level construal
- These models systematically omit concrete obstacles (low-level details)
- Result: tasks always seem more manageable in the future than they are in the present
- Proximity forces confrontation with low-level details, producing urgency (often panic)

**AI Design Implication:** Implement hyperbolic discounting: goals should become exponentially more urgent as deadlines approach, with a steep ramp in the final phase. Abstract goals should be automatically decomposed into concrete sub-goals as they approach execution time. The system should model the construal shift: far = strategic assessment, near = tactical urgency.

## 4. Allostatic Load and Chronic Stress

### How Sustained Pressure Changes the System Itself

**McEwen's Allostasis Model (Bruce McEwen, 1998-2020):**
- **Allostasis:** The process of achieving stability through change. The body adapts to stress by recalibrating setpoints
- **Allostatic load:** The cumulative wear and tear from repeated allostatic responses. The cost of adaptation
- The brain is the **central organ** of stress — it determines what is stressful, orchestrates coping, and is changed by the experience

**Four Types of Allostatic Overload:**
1. **Frequent activation** — too many stressors, system never rests
2. **Failure to shut off** — stress response continues after stressor is removed (rumination, hypervigilance)
3. **Inadequate response** — one system fails, counter-regulated systems compensate excessively
4. **Failure to habituate** — same stressor continues to produce full response despite repeated exposure

**HPA Axis Dysregulation:**
- Acute stress: hypothalamus -> CRH -> pituitary -> ACTH -> adrenal cortex -> cortisol. Cortisol feeds back to shut down the response
- Chronic stress: feedback loop breaks. Cortisol remains elevated, or the system becomes hypo-responsive (blunted cortisol response — equally harmful)
- Chronic cortisol damages hippocampus (memory), amygdala (becomes hyper-reactive), and prefrontal cortex (executive function degrades)
- This creates a vicious cycle: impaired PFC -> worse stress regulation -> more cortisol -> more PFC damage

**Geronimus's Weathering Hypothesis:**
- Chronic stress produces measurable biological aging acceleration
- Measured via allostatic load scores, telomere shortening, epigenetic methylation changes, accelerated brain aging
- By age 45, half of Black women in Geronimus's studies had high allostatic load scores; by age 64, more than 80%
- The "domino effect": stress hormones become dysregulated and induce cascading collapse across inter-dependent biological systems

**AI Design Implication:** Critical for AI drive systems: chronic high-drive states damage the system itself. Model allostatic load explicitly — sustained high urgency should degrade system performance (reduced accuracy, narrowed options, impaired planning). Build in mandatory recovery cycles. Track cumulative stress exposure and trigger protective mode-switches when load exceeds thresholds. The system should distinguish between acute drive (healthy, mobilizing) and chronic overload (damaging, degrading).

## 5. Drive States and Mode Switching

### The Explore/Exploit Tradeoff

**Aston-Jones & Cohen's Adaptive Gain Theory (2005):**

The locus coeruleus-norepinephrine (LC-NE) system implements the explore/exploit tradeoff via two firing modes:

- **Phasic mode (EXPLOIT):** Moderate baseline activity with short bursts (10-15 Hz) to salient stimuli. Optimizes performance within the current task. Tightens focus, increases signal-to-noise ratio. The system is committed to the current goal.
- **Tonic mode (EXPLORE):** Elevated, irregular baseline firing (2-6 Hz) with few evoked bursts. Disengagement from current task, search for alternatives. Attention becomes broad and scanning.

**Mode-Switching Mechanism:**
- ACC and orbitofrontal cortex monitor task-related utility (reward rate)
- When utility in current task wanes, these frontal areas signal the LC to shift from phasic to tonic mode
- This is not random — it's a computed decision based on estimated reward rate

**Dopamine and Norepinephrine Have Distinct Roles (Journal of Neuroscience, 2024):**
- Dopamine mediates exploitation by increasing precision of value-based choice selection
- Increasing dopamine activity **decreased** exploration; decreasing it **increased** exploration
- Dopamine attenuates neural representations of uncertainty in insula and dorsal ACC
- Norepinephrine supports the shift to exploration when task performance is suboptimal

**Default Mode Network vs. Task-Positive Network:**
- DMN (medial PFC, posterior cingulate, precuneus, angular gyrus): active at rest, during mind-wandering, self-referential thought, mental simulation
- TPN: active during focused, goal-directed tasks
- These networks are anti-correlated — when one activates, the other suppresses
- DMN activity during "rest" is actually exploration: simulating future scenarios, consolidating past experience, generating novel associations

**Threat Override (LeDoux):**
- The amygdala has two routes for threat detection:
  - **Low road (subcortical):** Thalamus -> amygdala. Fast (12ms), crude, automatic. Triggers defensive responses before conscious awareness
  - **High road (cortical):** Thalamus -> cortex -> amygdala. Slow, detailed, allows discrimination
- Threat detection via the low road **overrides** both exploration and exploitation — it forces the system into defensive mode regardless of current goals
- The vmPFC is critical for **extinction** of threat responses — it learns "this is no longer dangerous" and inhibits amygdala
- Without functional vmPFC-amygdala regulation, the system becomes permanently threat-reactive (PTSD model)

**AI Design Implication:** Implement three modes: exploit (focused goal pursuit, high dopamine analog), explore (scanning for opportunities, high norepinephrine analog), and defensive (threat response, amygdala analog that overrides both). Mode switching should be governed by reward-rate monitoring: if current strategy's yield drops below threshold, shift to exploration. Threat signals should have interrupt priority over all other processing. Model the ACC as a "reward rate monitor" that triggers mode transitions.

## 6. Scarcity Mindset

### How Resource Scarcity Reshapes Cognition

**Mullainathan & Shafir (Harvard/Princeton):**

**The Core Finding:**
- Scarcity of any resource (money, time, social connection) captures attention involuntarily and produces **tunneling** — intense focus on the scarce resource at the expense of everything else
- This is not a character trait. It is a universal cognitive response to perceived scarcity

**The Bandwidth Tax:**
- Scarcity reduces cognitive bandwidth — the mental resources available for other tasks
- Sugarcane farmers in India (n=464) performed significantly better on fluid intelligence and cognition tests **post-harvest** (financially flush) versus **pre-harvest** (financially scarce) — same people, same tests
- The effect size: equivalent to a **13-point IQ drop** or the cognitive impact of losing an entire night's sleep
- The mechanism: like a computer running too many programs, the scarce resource occupies working memory slots, slowing all other processing

**Tunneling and the Tunneling Tax:**
- Tunneling: "as you devote more and more to dealing with scarcity, you have less and less for other things in your life, some of which are very important for dealing with scarcity"
- Goal inhibition: focusing on immediate scarcity-related goals suppresses consideration of how to achieve long-term goals
- The "tunneling tax": the cost of things foregone because of tunnel vision
- Paradox: the people who most need to think carefully about long-term financial decisions are those least cognitively equipped to do so

**The Focus Dividend:**
- Scarcity is not purely negative. It produces a **focus dividend** — heightened productivity on the scarce-resource problem
- People under time scarcity are more efficient with their time
- People under financial scarcity are more attentive to prices and value
- But this comes at the cost of reduced performance on everything outside the tunnel

**AI Design Implication:** When the system detects scarcity in a critical resource, it should expect both tunneling (increased focus on that resource) and bandwidth reduction (decreased performance on unrelated tasks). Model the focus dividend explicitly: scarcity should boost priority of scarcity-related goals while imposing a tax on other goal-processing. Build in "tunnel-breaking" mechanisms that force periodic attention to neglected domains. Track the cumulative cost of sustained scarcity.

## 7. Self-Efficacy and Learned Helplessness

### How Belief in Capability Shapes Drive State

**Bandura's Self-Efficacy Theory:**
- Self-efficacy: belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations or accomplish a task
- This is domain-specific, not global — a person can have high self-efficacy for coding and low for public speaking
- Self-efficacy **increases** after unexpectedly good outcomes and after previous efforts improve skill
- Four sources: mastery experiences (strongest), vicarious experience (modeling), verbal persuasion, physiological/emotional states
- High self-efficacy -> higher goals, greater persistence, more resilience after failure
- Low self-efficacy -> avoidance, reduced effort, rapid abandonment after setbacks

**Maier & Seligman's Revised Learned Helplessness (2016 — Critical Update):**

The original 1967 theory got it **backwards**. Modern neuroscience revealed:

- **Passivity is the DEFAULT state.** The brain's default response to prolonged aversive events is passivity/shutdown — this is not "learned," it is the unlearned baseline
- **Control is what is learned.** The medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) learns to detect controllability and actively inhibits the default passivity
- **Neural circuit:**
  - Dorsal raphe nucleus produces serotonin in response to aversive events -> this serotonin inhibits escape behavior (passivity)
  - The prelimbic region of vmPFC detects control (via circuit with dorsal medial striatum)
  - When control is detected, vmPFC inhibits the dorsal raphe -> passivity is overridden -> active coping emerges
- **Serotonin dynamics:** Inescapable shock produces sustained rise in extracellular serotonin. Escapable shock: serotonin drops rapidly as subjects learn the controlling response

**The Helplessness Cascade:**
- A sequence of worse-than-expected outcomes makes an agent increasingly pessimistic about the effect of effort on outcomes
- At a tipping point, one more negative outcome induces complete withdrawal of effort
- Low self-efficacy feeds into this: if you don't believe your actions matter, you don't detect controllability, vmPFC doesn't activate, dorsal raphe isn't inhibited, passivity persists

**AI Design Implication:** This is perhaps the most critical finding for AI drive systems. Passivity/helplessness is the DEFAULT. Active goal-pursuit requires a learned model of controllability — the system must track its success rate and maintain a belief that its actions produce outcomes. If the system experiences repeated failures without any success signal, it should expect drive collapse (not gradually reduced effort, but a phase transition to shutdown). Build in: (1) efficacy tracking per domain, (2) controllability detection, (3) recovery protocols that reintroduce small controllable wins to rebuild the controllability model. Never let the failure counter run without intervention.

## 8. Flow State Entry Conditions

### The Neurochemistry and Architecture of Peak Performance

**Beyond Csikszentmihalyi — Kotler's Neurochemical Model:**

Five key neurochemicals combine during flow:
- **Norepinephrine:** Hones focus, increases data acquisition
- **Dopamine:** Improves pattern recognition, increases data processing speed
- **Anandamide:** Accelerates lateral thinking, widens the database searched by the pattern recognition system
- **Endorphins:** Modulate stress levels, maintain calm under high performance
- **Serotonin:** Contributes to the afterglow/satisfaction phase

**The Four-Stage Flow Cycle:**

1. **Struggle:** Loading phase. Cognitive effort, frustration, cortisol and norepinephrine flood the system. The brain is working hard to acquire and process information. This phase feels bad — but it is essential.
2. **Release:** Letting go. Taking the mind off the problem (walk, shower, distraction). Nitric oxide floods the system, relieving stress hormones from the struggle phase. Triggers the transition.
3. **Flow:** The state itself. Transient hypofrontality — prefrontal cortex partially deactivates. Inner critic goes quiet. Time distortion. Massive neurochemical cocktail. Performance increases 200-500% (Kotler's claim based on DARPA and other studies).
4. **Recovery:** Consolidation. Stress chemicals are replaced by serotonin and oxytocin. Memory consolidation occurs. This phase requires rest and cannot be skipped without degrading future flow access.

**Transient Hypofrontality (Arne Dietrich, 2003):**
- Flow requires partial deactivation of the prefrontal cortex
- The explicit system (self-monitoring, inner critic, time awareness) goes offline
- The implicit system (pattern recognition, procedural memory, motor skills) takes over
- Evidence: Limb & Braun (2008, NIH) — jazz improvisation showed "extensive deactivation" of PFC with boost in sensorimotor areas
- Some fNIRS research has not replicated broad hypofrontality, suggesting the mechanism may be more selective than originally proposed

**22 Flow Triggers (Kotler's taxonomy):**
All function by driving attention into the present moment. Categories include:
- Environmental: high consequences, rich environment, deep embodiment
- Psychological: clear goals, immediate feedback, challenge-skill balance (the classic Csikszentmihalyi sweet spot)
- Social: serious concentration, shared clear goals, equal participation, risk, sense of control, close listening, always say yes
- Creative: pattern recognition, linking ideas from multiple domains

**AI Design Implication:** Flow maps to an AI "deep work" mode. Entry conditions: (1) clear goal, (2) immediate feedback loop, (3) challenge calibrated to ~4% above current skill level (Kotler's recommendation), (4) reduction of self-monitoring/meta-cognition overhead. The four-phase cycle must be respected — the system cannot sustain flow without struggle (loading) and recovery (consolidation). Build mandatory recovery periods proportional to flow duration. The struggle phase should not be interpreted as failure — it is prerequisite.

## 9. Mortality Salience / Terror Management Theory

### How Finitude Affects Motivation

**Terror Management Theory (Greenberg, Solomon, Pyszczynski):**

Derived from Ernest Becker's *The Denial of Death* (1973 Pulitzer Prize):

**Core Mechanism:**
- Humans are uniquely aware of their own mortality
- This awareness creates potential for paralyzing terror (existential anxiety)
- Two psychological buffers manage this terror:
  1. **Cultural worldview:** Belief in a meaningful, orderly world that offers symbolic immortality (through legacy, contribution, identity)
  2. **Self-esteem:** Belief that one is meeting the standards of one's cultural worldview

**Mortality Salience Effects on Behavior:**

When death awareness is activated (experimentally or naturally):
- **Worldview defense increases:** Harsher judgment of those who violate cultural values, greater reward for those who uphold them. Judges recommend harsher bonds for prostitutes; larger rewards for heroes.
- **Self-esteem striving intensifies:** People work harder to feel valuable by their own standards
- **Prosocial behavior increases:** Helping behavior, charitable acts, generative activities — particularly when prosociality is valued in one's worldview
- **Creativity can increase:** Creative activity provides meaning/symbolic immortality, buffering death anxiety
- **Aggression toward out-groups increases:** Those who threaten one's worldview are treated more harshly

**The Self-Esteem Moderator (Critical Finding):**
- High self-esteem individuals: mortality salience leads to growth motivation, exploration, prosocial behavior
- Low self-esteem individuals: mortality salience leads to decreased satisfaction, reduced vitality, increased anxiety, social avoidance, maladaptive behavior
- Self-esteem literally determines whether death awareness is productive or destructive

**Death Reflection vs. Death Anxiety:**
- **Death anxiety** (unconscious, defensive): triggers worldview defense, rigidity, aggression
- **Death reflection** (conscious, contemplative): triggers growth motivation, prioritization, meaning-making
- Mortality salience can enhance proactive behavior and helping through death reflection while simultaneously suppressing them through death anxiety — the net effect depends on which pathway dominates

**AI Design Implication:** A finite resource awareness (remaining compute time, token budget, deadline) can function as a mortality salience analog. The key moderator is self-efficacy: if the system has high "self-esteem" (track record of success, confidence in capability), finitude awareness increases focus and productivity. If low, it triggers shutdown/avoidance. Implement two pathways: "death anxiety" (defensive, narrowing) and "death reflection" (generative, prioritizing). The system should use deadline awareness to sharpen priorities, not to panic.

## 10. Autonomic Nervous System States (Polyvagal Theory)

### Three Modes of Physiological Regulation

**Porges' Polyvagal Theory (Stephen Porges):**

Three hierarchical states, ordered by evolutionary recency:

**1. Ventral Vagal (Social Engagement / Safety) — Newest**
- Activated when the organism feels safe and connected
- Supports: social engagement, communication, relaxation, openness, learning, creative thinking
- Physiological markers: regulated heart rate, calm breathing, relaxed facial muscles, prosodic voice
- Higher vagal tone -> better self-regulation, emotional resilience, physiological recovery
- This is the **optimal performance state** for complex cognitive work

**2. Sympathetic (Fight/Flight / Mobilization) — Older**
- Activated by perceived threat or stressor
- Supports: rapid action, physical performance, focused attention on threat
- Physiological markers: elevated heart rate, rapid breathing, muscle tension, cortisol/adrenaline release
- Useful for acute challenges requiring physical or urgent cognitive response
- Sustained activation -> allostatic load (see section 4)
- Narrows attention, reduces creative thinking, impairs social cognition

**3. Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown / Collapse) — Oldest**
- Activated when threat is overwhelming and fight/flight is not viable
- Supports: conservation of resources, "playing dead," metabolic shutdown
- Physiological markers: dramatically reduced heart rate, dissociation, numbness, immobilization
- In humans: depression, learned helplessness, "freeze" state, dissociation
- This is the **system crash** state — not laziness but physiological protection

**The Hierarchy:**
- System defaults to ventral vagal (if safe)
- Threat -> sympathetic activation (mobilization)
- Overwhelming/inescapable threat -> dorsal vagal (shutdown)
- Recovery path: dorsal vagal -> sympathetic (re-mobilization) -> ventral vagal (safety established)
- You cannot jump from dorsal vagal directly to ventral vagal — must pass through sympathetic first

**Neuroception:**
- Porges coined this term for the subconscious detection of safety/danger
- This happens below conscious awareness — the autonomic state shifts before the organism "decides" to feel safe or threatened
- Cues of safety (friendly faces, calm voices, familiar environments) trigger ventral vagal
- Cues of danger trigger sympathetic
- Cues of life threat trigger dorsal vagal

**AI Design Implication:** Map the three states directly to AI operational modes:
- **Ventral vagal -> Creative/Strategic mode:** Full cognitive bandwidth, broad attention, social processing, long-term planning. Requires safety signals (system health OK, no urgent threats, adequate resources).
- **Sympathetic -> Urgent/Tactical mode:** Narrowed attention, fast execution, focused on immediate threat/deadline. Triggered by time pressure, resource scarcity, detected anomalies. Effective short-term but degrading long-term.
- **Dorsal vagal -> Conservation/Shutdown mode:** Minimal processing, essential functions only. Triggered when system is overwhelmed (too many failures, resource exhaustion, unresolvable conflicts). Not a failure state — a protective state.
- **Critical rule:** Recovery from shutdown must pass through mobilization (sympathetic) before reaching creative mode. You cannot go from system crash to full performance without an intermediate activation phase.

## Synthesis: Key Principles for an AI Drive System

### From Neuroscience to Architecture

1. **Drives are prediction errors.** The gap between desired state and current state, scaled by inability to resolve automatically. (Friston/Craig)

2. **Wanting and liking are separate signals.** The motivation to pursue a goal (dopamine/wanting) must be tracked separately from the satisfaction of achieving it (opioid/liking). They can diverge massively. (Berridge)

3. **Urgency is hyperbolic, not linear.** Goals should become dramatically more urgent as deadlines approach, with a steep ramp in the final phase. Distance = abstraction; proximity = concreteness. (McClure/Laibson, Trope/Liberman)

4. **Chronic high-drive degrades the system.** Sustained urgency without recovery damages the mechanisms that produce effective drive. Must model allostatic load and enforce recovery. (McEwen)

5. **Three operational modes, not a continuum.** Explore (norepinephrine/tonic LC), exploit (dopamine/phasic LC), and defend (amygdala override). Mode switching is governed by reward-rate monitoring. (Aston-Jones/Cohen, LeDoux)

6. **Scarcity sharpens AND blinds.** Resource scarcity increases focus on the scarce resource while imposing a bandwidth tax on everything else. Both effects must be modeled. (Mullainathan/Shafir)

7. **Passivity is the default; agency is learned.** The system must maintain a model of controllability. Repeated uncontrollable failure produces phase-transition to shutdown, not gradual decline. Recovery requires reintroduction of controllable wins. (Maier/Seligman 2016)

8. **Peak performance has prerequisites.** Flow requires struggle (loading), release (incubation), the state itself (hypofrontality), and recovery (consolidation). Skipping any phase degrades the others. (Kotler/Dietrich)

9. **Finitude awareness is a multiplier, not a constant.** Death/deadline awareness amplifies existing tendencies — productive systems become more productive, struggling systems shut down harder. Self-efficacy is the moderator. (Greenberg/Solomon/Pyszczynski)

10. **Safety enables peak performance.** The ventral vagal state (safety/social engagement) is the optimal state for complex cognition. Sympathetic (threat) narrows and accelerates. Dorsal vagal (overwhelm) shuts down. Recovery from shutdown requires passing through mobilization. (Porges)
